Xiqu: Modern interpretation brings new life to ancient art form

Hong Kong’s Xiqu Centre gives ancient Chinese opera a new future

Chinese opera is a much-loved pastime for the older generation in Hong Kong. But a new traditional Chinese opera house nearing completion in the city’s West Kowloon Cultural District aims to shake up how Chinese people view the ancient art form and grow appreciation among an international audience.

The “Xiqu Centre”, as the new facility is called, has been inspired by the Chinese umbrella term “xiqu”, which refers to traditional Chinese opera styles that span Kunqu, Cantonese and Peking. Yet its architecture is distinctly modern, with a soaring steel building that resembles the opening curtains on a stage that’s designed to breathe new life into the UNESCO-recognised cultural performance.

“Young people think Cantonese opera is old-fashioned and they don’t feel close to the art; that is why we need to build the Xiqu Centre,” says the Executive Director for Performing Arts of West Kowloon Cultural District Authority, Louis Yu.

“We face the same problem as opera in the Western world—there are a lot of grey hairs. We want to give a new image to this traditional art form, like what is happening with Shakespeare’s plays in the West,” he explains.

While the centre will focus on Cantonese opera, its remit will encompass all styles of the traditional Chinese operas when it opens later in 2018, blending art forms like music, literature, martial arts, dance and juggling.

"The location of the Xiqu Centre and its connection to the city have a purpose—to keep Cantonese opera in the daily life of Hong Kong people,” adds Mr Yu. “It is the only intangible heritage on a world-class level that belongs to us.”

A new space
for a new generation

With the entertainment industry now dominated by social media, mobile gaming and streaming services, Chinese opera can feel static in contrast. The Royal Shakespeare Company (RSC) in the UK, for example, runs digital content via its RSC Live Collection, reaching out to a new generation of theatre-goers. Hong Kong’s existing Cantonese opera venues such as the Ko Shan Theatre, Sunbeam Theatre and Yau Ma Tei Theatre remain popular among the city’s older generation, but lack the space to deliver a fresh impact to a new generation.

“There is a generation that lost the roots of Cantonese opera, and having the Xiqu Centre is part of a journey to search for your own identity"

BRYANT LU (RONALD LU & PARTNERS)

Led by Hong Kong-born architects Bing Thom Architects and Ronald Lu & Partners, the building features a lantern-style design, with an open atrium and eight floors that span 13,800 square metres. Designed around the concept of flow, the Centre’s position on Canton Road, close to the city’s Jordan and Austin MTR stations, makes it the eastern gateway to the new West Kowloon Cultural District.

“There is a generation that lost the roots of Cantonese opera, and having the Xiqu Centre is part of a journey to search for your own identity, giving us a stronger connection to the city, and a history, identity and legacy,” says Bryant Lu, Vice Chairman, Ronald Lu & Partners.

The atrium offers a vocal point and venue for arts and crafts and street performers. On the first floor is a 760-square-metre, 200-seat Tea House Theatre for small shows, where visitors can enjoy opera with dim sum and tea. On the top floor, the Grand Theatre offers 1,073 seats in a world-leading setting.

“Bing Thom, who has now sadly passed away, saw Xiqu as a flow, and the building follows with curved design and natural ventilation. He discussed attracting young people as crucial, so we lifted the theatre up and created a public plaza. We wanted a covered public space serving as a natural gathering point to rejuvenate and modernise this art form,” says Mr Yu.

A new space
for a new generation

The design of the main stage has deeply considered the acoustics of Chinese opera and provides large-sized doors to accommodate full opera dress. It also has an expanded back stage and an orchestra pit that can go up and down, allowing the stage to come closer when needed. Outside the main theatre, there is a floor dedicated to learning, a seminar hall, and eight individual studios for master classes, culture exchange, training and rehearsals.

“This is the first time for Chinese opera house has been purpose-built, so we are setting an international standard,” Mr Yu explains.

Across the world, the ‘Bilboa effect’ is being felt strongly, and with creative industries flourishing, culture has become a strong economic driver. “In the past 30 years, the world has recognised that culture plays a more important role in economics, and people see the linkages between museums, opera, classical music and the creative economy. It all started with the Guggenheim Museum in Bilboa. Now, look at Dubai, Doha, Shanghai and many others – they are all developing an experience-based creative economy,” says Mr Yu.

“We aim to build a sense of belonging, and make traditional opera hip again.”

LOUIS YU, Executive Director for Performing Arts of West Kowloon Cultural District

Aside from a new home, fostering talent and creativity on the ground is essential to bring Chinese opera into the 21st Century. The Xiqu Centre aims to develop this by collaborating with local schools, hosting an in-house troupe, and attracting international tourists with guided shows at the Tea House Theatre.

"We want to position this as a centre of traditional culture in Honk Kong, says Mr Yu." We aim to build a sens of belonging, and make traditional opera hip again.""
